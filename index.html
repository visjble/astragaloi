History & Method

What Are the Astragaloi?

Astragaloi — knucklebones of sheep or goats — were among the most sacred objects in ancient Greece. Before there were dice, before there were coins, there were bones. They were cast at shrines, in homes, at the threshold of decisions that could not be made by reason alone.

Each bone has four distinct faces, each with a traditional name and numerical value: the dog-throw (κῷος, 1), the twisted face (χῖος, 3), the flat side (πλευρός, 4), and the back — the most fortunate face (ὕπτιος, 6). Four bones thrown together produce combinations that were mapped, in ancient inscriptions, to divine responses.

Astragalomancy inscriptions — stone tablets listing every combination and its oracle — survive from sanctuaries across Asia Minor and Greece. This practice was not superstition. It was a structured method of consulting the divine: a technology for thinking through what could not be thought through alone.

"We set aside the bones for the gods — they speak back through the way they fall." — paraphrase of Plato, Phaedrus 244b–c, on divination and divine gift
  • 1
    κῷος — Koos The concave side. The lowest value. Called "the dog" in gaming contexts — a throw of ill fortune.
  • 3
    χῖος — Khios The convex side. A middling face, associated with the shape of the letter chi — curved and crossing.
  • 4
    πλευρός — Pleuros The broad flat face. Steady and solid. The side that shows the rib-plane of the bone.
  • 6
    ὕπτιος — Hyptios The back — the most favorable face. In Roman tradition this became the "Venus throw." The bone lying open, face upward.
